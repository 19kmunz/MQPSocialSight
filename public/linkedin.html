Individual Professional Presentation Platform

Users: 310.6 million
Annual Revenue: $13.8 billion

Key Strengths
  1. Work-focused profiles for professionals
  2. Ease of applications for jobs
  3. Authentic personas
Key Warnings
  1. No financial support for creators
  2. Posts are mostly achievement based or for a “professional” persona
  3. Strongly advertiser based

Design Overview

LinkedIn features an infinitely scrollable feed of image, text and video posts by people you are connected to and posts they have liked. This feed can be sorted by default "Top" or a sorted "Recent" categories. You can find connections within your school or work organizations. All profiles will be either 1st degree (your connections), 2nd degree (connected to your connections) or 3rd+ degree connections (external).
LinkedIn also features a paginated feed of jobs, including jobs recommended by your profile information

Create a Professional Profile

LinkedIn encourages you to create a detailed profile of yourself to present to colleagues and employers. This includes a profile picture, education, work experience, and skills.

Apply To Jobs

You can search for open jobs by title, skill, company, or location. Once you find a job, you can view the description and apply or save the job to apply later.

Infinitely Scrolling Feed

Your feed is infinite, meaning that there will never be a second page. The feed combines the activity of people you follow with content recommended by an algorithm.


Analysis

Below is our analysis of LinkedIn based on survey data gathered by participants.

This section seeks to compare LinkedIn's ability to build and support communities. In general, users agree that LinkedIn generally encourages positive communities, but it is difficult to maintain friendships. Click the dropdown to see specifics.

On average people frequently see positive conversation on LinkedIn. Although most users occasionally see this, many see positive conversation very frequently. As a professional setting, LinkedIn will be polite and may congratulate others on achievements.

People very rarely see negativity on LinkedIn. Since this is a professional environment, politeness is encouraged.

People occasionally see views that differ from theirs, which may lead to debate.

Although LinkedIn is definitely a platform to connect with coworkers, your feed may have a mix of corporate and personal posts. Respondents tend to agree that the feed tends to prioritize connections, but not as strongly as places like Instagram or BeReal. Once again, we are unsure if our respondents tend to use the feed or just the profile features, so these answers may be skewed.

People tend to slightly agree that this prioritizes sponsored posts over other posts. While sponsorship is common, other posts are still very much visible.

Most people occasionally see two-way discussion on LinkedIn, with most responses between being frequent and rare. This may be because LinkedIn is a professional environment with users discussing topics such as new jobs, research and new ideas.

In general, people lean towards slightly agreeing that it is easy to make new friends on LinkedIn. This may be because LinkedIn recommends people within the same organizations, so they may suggest people you know or have similar interest to.

This section seeks to understand whether individual identities are encouraged by LinkedIn, which users overwhelmingly agree on. LinkedIn is a positive place to build a professional profile. Click the dropdown to see specifics.

People agree that users feel like real individuals, but slightly less than Facebook. This may be due to LinkedIn's professional nature and the frequency of recruiters who use automated messages to reach potential candidates.

People tend to agree, especially since LinkedIn revolves around a user's professional identity. LinkedIn is generally used in academic and professional environments, so users are required to present an identity to peers.

People strongly agree that their LinkedIn personas are authentic. This is how they present themselves to coworkers and peers, so it will relate to themselves.

Users strongly that LinkedIn enables profile customization. This is one of LinkedIn's key features, as your profile showcases your professional identity and contains your skills and experiences.

Users agree that they curate their profiles. This is the main feature of LinkedIn and profiles are emphasized to other users, so curating a profile is important.

This section targets the value of content on LinkedIn, including its high educational value, as well as ease to create new content. Click the dropdown to see specifics.

Users say that they see posts by small creators occasionally, since people follow peers and professionals but may see posts from large organizations.

Most people spend less than 10 minutes on LinkedIn. Users may be checking for updates, sending quick messages, or briefly updating their profiles.

Most people believe LinkedIn prioritizes text content. This includes status updates, research and job postings.

People agree that content is easy to create, since social media is designed to encourage posting.

People are not often entertained by LinkedIn. Although the highest number of respondents said they are occasionally entertained, responses lean negative. Content is education-based and professional, used for networking, which may make it dry.

People are often educated by content on LinkedIn, with most respondents answering frequently or occasionally. LinkedIn is for professionals and academics, thus posts will often contain educational information about research or a new discovery.

Infrequent LinkedIn users primarily use it as a digital resume, rather than for its content. The platform's corporate culture inherently makes the content less "entertaining" than its contemporaries, leading to occasional user boredom, but this is expected and some users are satisfied with that.

As a professional environment, users tend to be careful about what they post. People tend to post about their professional successes or new relevant to their field, and nothing that makes them look bad. Although misinformation is still present on the platform, the culture limits discussion and spread.

Here, we seek to understand how much user control LinkedIn encourages. This includes moderation and control of advertisements. Responses agree that LinkedIn allows for some user control, but answers vary. Click the dropdown to see specifics.

Survey respondents may only user profile features, so the survey responses may not accurately reflect the feed. Overall, posts will be recommended if posted or liked by a connection, so your feed will be linked to your friend's choices.

LinkedIn makes $3 billion dollars annually off of its ad spots. There are settings to disable personalized ads and a premium model, which may be why our participants lean towards slightly disagree over strongly disagree, but this may just as well be because our survey respondents may primarily use profile features, when ads show up in the feed.

LinkedIn is not known for a strong recommendation algorithm. The feed will mostly be comprised of posts your connections have likes or companies you have followed, not recommended content. The professional attitude means much of the content will not align with hobbies, but may align with your professional interests.

Once again, the culture of professionalism keeps shocking content to a minimum.

LinkedIn lets you privatize almost everything on your profile, with granular preference based on degrees of connection to a person. The business focused culture keep interactions clean and professional, so issues like bullying and hate speech are often mitigated by the culture.

LinkedIn content is business focused, so it won't be revisited for entertainment purposes as often as other media. There are features for saving posts and jobs, but those rely on user action, and it;s search features aid primarily in finding pages and current chatter, not revisiting a post. Our users may primarily use profile features, so this data may be skewed.
